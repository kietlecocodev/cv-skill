# Product Manager Tech CV Template

## Target Roles

Product manager, technical product manager, platform PM, growth PM, AI product manager, product owner in tech organizations.

## When to Use

Use when the JD emphasizes product discovery, roadmap ownership, customer/user insight, experiments, metrics, stakeholder alignment, technical fluency, platform/API products, or go-to-market coordination.

## Section Order

1. Header
2. Product positioning summary
3. Core product skills
4. Product experience
5. Selected launches/case studies
6. Education/certifications

## Reconstructed Skeleton

### Summary

Product manager with <years> years driving <product/domain> outcomes across <customer/user segment>. Strong in <discovery/analytics/platform/GTM> with experience partnering with engineering, design, data, and business teams to ship <type of product impact>.

### Core Skills

- Product: discovery, roadmap, prioritization, experimentation, launch planning
- Analytics: SQL, funnels, retention, activation, cohort analysis, dashboards
- Technical: APIs, platform products, data workflows, AI/ML concepts, developer experience
- Collaboration: stakeholder management, executive communication, cross-functional delivery

### Experience Bullet Pattern

- Led <product initiative> from <discovery/research> to launch, improving <activation/retention/revenue/adoption> by <number>.
- Prioritized roadmap across <stakeholders/constraints>, shipping <feature/platform> used by <users/customers/internal teams>.
- Partnered with engineering/data/design to validate <hypothesis>, reducing <risk/friction/churn/manual work>.

## Bullet Style

Tie product decisions to customer evidence and business metrics. Show how technical fluency improved trade-offs, scoping, or adoption without pretending to be the engineer unless the candidate truly built the system.

## Optimization Goal

Show product judgment, customer empathy, metric ownership, technical collaboration, and ability to turn ambiguity into shipped outcomes.

## Pitfalls

- Describing meetings instead of decisions and outcomes.
- Listing frameworks without evidence of product impact.
- Overusing vague words like strategic, collaborative, and data-driven.
- Claiming technical depth not supported by actual work.
