# Seniority Signals

## Purpose

Make candidate level visible through scope, ambiguity, ownership, influence, and outcomes.

## What Good Looks Like

Seniority should be proven through work evidence, not labels. Show how the candidate handled bigger scope, fewer instructions, higher stakes, or broader influence.

## Weak vs Strong

Weak: Senior engineer responsible for architecture.

Strong: Led migration from monolith checkout service to event-driven services across 4 teams, reducing peak incident rate by 32% while keeping release downtime under 10 minutes.

Weak: Managed engineers.

Strong: Managed 9 engineers across platform and product squads, hiring 4 senior engineers and improving quarterly roadmap delivery from 62% to 86% within two planning cycles.

## Role-Specific Notes

- Junior: learning speed, project completion, code quality, collaboration.
- Mid: independent delivery, ownership of features/services, debugging, reliable execution.
- Senior: system ownership, trade-offs, mentoring, cross-team influence, incident ownership.
- Staff/principal: technical strategy, architecture direction, org-level leverage, standards.
- Manager: hiring, coaching, team health, delivery, prioritization, stakeholder alignment.

## Common Mistakes

- Using senior titles without scope evidence.
- Confusing years of experience with seniority.
- Ignoring mentorship or influence.
- Claiming strategy when work was task execution.
